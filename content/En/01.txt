Mitchell Hashimoto’s New Way of Writing Code
From a Self-Taught Programmer to the Founder of HashiCorp: A Journey That Changed Cloud Infrastructure

Mitchell Hashimoto is the co-founder of HashiCorp and the creator of well-known open-source tools such as Terraform, Vagrant, and Vault. His tools have fundamentally changed how modern cloud infrastructure is managed. In this interview, he reflects on his programming journey, the birth of HashiCorp, and how open source, cloud computing, and AI are shaping the future of software engineering.

This article follows the structure of the interview and walks through Mitchell’s experiences and insights.

1. The Beginning: A 12-Year-Old Teaching Himself Programming

Mitchell’s programming journey started very early.

He began teaching himself programming at around 12 or 13 years old. Like many programmers, his initial motivation was video games.

But very quickly he discovered that he preferred web development. At that time the internet was still very young—Google didn’t even exist yet. He started learning:

PHP

Perl

Basic web development

Because he was young and didn’t have money to buy books, he mainly learned by studying open-source code online.

One story from that time is particularly memorable.

He printed out the first few chapters of the PHP manual (around 30–40 pages) and read them repeatedly while walking to school every day. One day, he suddenly realized that the $ symbol in PHP represented variables.

That moment became a turning point.

Once he understood variables, his learning speed accelerated dramatically, and he was finally able to write real programs.

2. Teenage Years: Freelancing, Copying Websites, and Earning His First Money

By the time he was in high school, Mitchell was already coding every day.

He built many things, including:

Gaming websites

Online forums

Clones of existing web features

Even experiments replicating parts of PayPal’s payment system

He also did something bold:

He pretended to be 18 years old on freelancing websites to take on jobs.

Some of the projects he worked on included:

Image upload systems

Small websites

Each project might only pay $50 or $100, but it allowed him to start earning money with programming.

3. Programming Was a Lonely Path

Back then, being interested in computers was not considered cool.

Mitchell once said:

If you liked computers back then, it was basically social suicide.

Because of this:

He never talked about programming at school

It was almost like a secret life

However, he found a community online through:

MSN Messenger

AOL Messenger

Various forums

Many of those online friends eventually became long-term friendships.

4. His First Real Job: Ruby on Rails

While studying at the University of Washington, he received an email that changed his life.

Someone asked him:

“Would you like to become a Ruby on Rails engineer?”

At the time, he only knew PHP and had no experience with Ruby.

But he still accepted the job.

The company was a Rails consulting firm that specialized in building MVPs for clients.

During that time he worked on projects such as:

YouTube-style websites

E-commerce platforms

Charity platforms

This experience exposed him to many different technologies and product ideas very quickly.

5. A Strange Mentor Who Changed His Direction

At that company there was a very mysterious engineer.

His characteristics:

Long hair

Never used a mouse

Worked entirely in Linux

Sat quietly in a corner

Mitchell became very curious about him.

One day, that engineer did something unusual:

He unplugged Mitchell’s mouse.

Then he said:

“You’re not allowed to use a mouse anymore.”

Mitchell spent a week learning how to operate his computer entirely with the keyboard.

After that he began learning tools such as:

Terminal

Screen

SSH

Linux package managers

This was the moment he started entering the world of infrastructure engineering.

6. A Failed University Research Project

During college he joined a research project called the Seattle Project.

The goal was to build something similar to:

Folding@Home

A distributed computing network

The idea was that computers around the world could contribute computing power.

Mitchell’s responsibility was to build the node management and infrastructure system.

But he completely failed.

However, that failure became extremely important.

In his notebook he wrote down the reasons he couldn’t complete the project:

He couldn’t manage resources declaratively

He couldn’t build private networks easily

He couldn’t manage distributed nodes

These problems later became the product blueprint for HashiCorp.

7. An Email That Started a Company

His research supervisor, Arman, read that notebook.

A few weeks later he sent Mitchell an email:

“Do you want to start a company together?”

Mitchell replied within two minutes:

“Yes.”

And that was the beginning of HashiCorp.

8. The First Successful Product: Vagrant

While working at the consulting company, Mitchell noticed a recurring problem.

Every project required a different development environment.

Setting up a development environment could take half a day.

So he built a tool called Vagrant.

The goal was simple:

“Double-click and your development environment is ready.”

It was built using:

VirtualBox

Virtual machines

This solved the problem of non-reproducible development environments.

9. The Arrival of the Cloud Era

Around 2010, AWS had just been introduced.

At the time:

EC2 was very unstable

There were very few services

Only S3 was considered reliable

But Mitchell felt something important:

This is the future.

Not because of market analysis, but because it simply felt like a better way to build systems.

10. The HashiCorp Product Ecosystem

Over time HashiCorp launched a series of tools.

Vagrant

Development environment management.

Packer

Machine image building.

Consul

Service discovery.

Terraform

Infrastructure as Code.

Instead of manually configuring infrastructure, you could write something like:

This text describes an entire AWS infrastructure

After executing it, Terraform could automatically create:

VPC

Subnets

Instances

Gateways

And it could also destroy everything with a single command.

Vault

Secrets management, such as:

Passwords

API keys

Credit card data

Personally identifiable information

Nomad

A job scheduler that automatically deploys applications across multiple machines.

11. The Struggle With Business Models

At the beginning, HashiCorp had no business model at all.

For the first four years:

They made almost no revenue

Everything was open source

Investors still supported them because they saw strong signals:

GitHub stars

Download numbers

Developer adoption

These metrics suggested product-market fit.

12. The First Failed Business Model

Their first commercial product was called Atlas.

But it failed.

The reason was that Atlas required companies to adopt all HashiCorp tools together.

Most companies didn’t want that.

There was also another problem.

Inside companies, nobody knew which department should pay.

For example:

Security teams

Networking teams

Infrastructure teams

Everyone kept passing the responsibility to someone else.

13. The Turning Point: Open Core

After a board meeting, the founders realized their strategy was wrong.

They decided to start over.

The new model was Open Core.

The core product remained open source

Enterprise features were closed source

In addition, each product became its own commercial offering.

The first successful enterprise product was Vault Enterprise.

Companies needed features such as:

Secrets replication

High availability

Enterprise support

This finally created a real revenue stream.

14. Why Terraform Became So Popular

Many people assume Terraform succeeded because it was first to market.

Mitchell says that’s not true.

Terraform was actually the seventh product in that space.

The real reason was relentless developer outreach.

He constantly:

Gave talks at conferences

Traveled around the world

Spoke directly with developers

At one point he traveled once every eight days for nine years.

He even coded on airplanes:

Downloading GitHub issues

Breaking them into 15-minute tasks

Solving them during flights

After landing he would push dozens of commits at once.

15. VMware Almost Acquired HashiCorp

Two years after founding the company, VMware attempted to acquire them.

The initial offer was $20 million.

Mitchell and Arman were only 23 years old.

It was a huge amount of money.

But they realized that if they sold the company, their dream might disappear.

So they counter-offered:

$100 million.

VMware’s board voted on it.

The deal failed by one vote.

If that vote had passed, Terraform might never have existed.

16. HashiCorp Goes Public

HashiCorp eventually went public in 2021.

Preparing for an IPO required:

More than a year of preparation

Practicing earnings calls

Extremely strict legal confidentiality

Even Mitchell’s parents didn’t know the company was about to go public.

17. His View on Cloud Giants

Mitchell mentioned that AWS sometimes felt arrogant.

There was a sense that:

“We could build a product tomorrow that destroys your company.”

But he also acknowledged something important.

AWS employs some of the best engineers in the world.

18. The Impact of AI on Open Source

Mitchell raised an interesting point about AI.

AI could potentially change how open-source communities function.

For example, AI systems can:

Automatically generate pull requests

Produce code that looks correct but may be wrong

Open source traditionally relies on trust.

In the future it might shift toward a default-deny model, where contributors must establish trust first.

Conclusion

Mitchell Hashimoto’s story represents a classic but highly influential technology entrepreneurship journey:

Self-taught programming

Discovering real infrastructure problems

Building open-source tools

Finding a sustainable business model

Transforming an entire industry

HashiCorp’s success did not come from being first.

It came from deeply focusing on real problems over a long period of time.

As Mitchell once said:

Great engineers don’t just write code — they understand systems and constraints.

And that mindset lies at the heart of modern software engineering.
